    Right to Education

    Background and Salient Features of the Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009

    (Exam-Ready Teacher Style Answer – 10 Marks, ~600 Words)

    The Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009 is a landmark legislation in the history of Indian education. It transformed elementary education from a Directive Principle into a Fundamental Right. The Act reflects the commitment of the Indian Constitution towards universal, equitable, and quality education for all children.

    1. Background of Enacting the RTE Act, 2009 (3:07:1)

    At the time of the adoption of the Indian Constitution in 1950, Article 45 under the Directive Principles of State Policy stated that:

    “The State shall endeavour to provide free and compulsory education for all children until they complete 14 years of age.”

    However, since Directive Principles are not legally enforceable, Article 45 remained only a direction and not a constitutional obligation. As a result, universal elementary education could not be fully achieved for several decades.

    To give legal status and enforceability to this provision, the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002 inserted Article 21A into the Constitution. According to Article 21A:

    “The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine.”

    Thus, education became a Fundamental Right.

    Following this amendment, the Parliament passed the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act on 4th August 2009, and the Act came into force on 1st April 2010, along with the necessary rules for implementation.

    2. Salient Features of the RTE Act, 2009 (3:07:2)

    The RTE Act contains several important provisions to ensure universal and quality elementary education.

    1. Free and Compulsory Education

    • Government (Central, State, and Local Bodies) must provide free and compulsory education up to Class VIII.
    • Schools must be available within 1 km (primary) and 3 km (upper primary) from the child’s residence.
    • Government must ensure admission, attendance, and completion of elementary education.
    • “Free education” includes free textbooks, uniforms, stationery, and materials for children with disabilities.
    • Funding responsibility is shared between Central and State governments.

    2. Benchmark Mandate for Schools

    The Act prescribes minimum norms and standards regarding:

    • Teacher-pupil ratio
    • Number of classrooms
    • Separate toilets for boys and girls
    • Safe drinking water
    • School working days and teacher working hours

    No school can function without meeting these standards.

    3. Special Provisions for Out-of-School Children

    Out-of-school children must be admitted to age-appropriate classes and provided special training to reach the required learning level.

    4. Quantity and Quality of Teachers

    • Teacher-Pupil Ratio prescribed (e.g., 1:40 maximum at primary level).
    • Minimum subject teachers required at upper primary level.
    • No disparity in PTR between rural and urban schools.
    • Teachers must possess required qualifications.
    • Teachers cannot be deployed for non-academic work except census, elections, and disaster relief.

    Duties of Teachers include:

    • Regularity and punctuality
    • Completing syllabus on time
    • Assessing students’ learning
    • Meeting parents regularly

    5. Zero Tolerance Against Discrimination

    • No screening tests for admission.
    • No capitation fee or donation (punishable offence).
    • No corporal punishment or mental harassment.
    • No discrimination based on caste, religion, gender, or economic status.
    • No birth certificate required for admission.

    6. Ensuring All-Round Development

    Curriculum must be in harmony with constitutional values. Teaching should be child-friendly and child-centred, ensuring development of knowledge, skills, talents, and emotional well-being.

    7. Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE)

    • No detention or expulsion before completion of elementary education.
    • Continuous assessment to improve learning outcomes.
    • Emphasis on both academic and co-curricular development.

    8. School Management Committees (SMC)

    • Mandatory formation in all schools.
    • 75% members must be parents.
    • 50% members must be women.
    • Responsible for monitoring school functioning and preparing development plans.

    9. Grievance Redressal Mechanism

    A Grievance Redressal Cell must be established to address complaints regarding non-compliance of RTE provisions.

    10. Social Inclusion (25% Reservation)

    Private unaided schools must reserve 25% seats for children from disadvantaged and economically weaker sections. The government reimburses the expenses.

    Conclusion

    The RTE Act, 2009 marks a historic step in achieving Universalisation of Elementary Education in India. By making education a fundamental right, prescribing quality standards, ensuring non-discrimination, and promoting social inclusion, the Act has significantly improved enrolment and access. Despite implementation challenges, it remains a milestone in ensuring free, compulsory, and quality education for every child in India.
